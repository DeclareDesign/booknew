13 Research Design Library

This section of the book enumerates a series of common social science research designs. Each entry will include description of the design in terms of M, I, D, and A and also a declaration of the design in code. We’ll often diagnose designs over the range of values of some design parameters in order to point out especially interesting or unusual features of the design.

Our goal in this section is not to provide a comprehensive accounting of all empirical research designs. It’s also not to describe any of the particular designs in exhaustive detail, because we are quite sure that in order for these designs to be useful for any practical purpose, they will need to be modified. The entries in the design library are not recipes that will automatically produce high-quality research. Instead, we hope that the entries provide inspiration for how to tailor a particular class of designs – the blocked-and-clustered randomized trial or the catch-and-release design – to your own research setting. The basic structure of the design library entry will be useful, but the specifics about plausible ranges of outcomes, sample size constraints, etc, will be different in each particular setting.

We’ve split up designs by inquiry and by data strategy. Inquiries can be descriptive or causal and data strategies can be observational or experimental. We describe four categories of research design: observational descriptive, experimental descriptive, observational Causal, and experimental causal. A third dimension along which studies can vary is whether the answer strategy is qualitative or quantitative. If we include this dimension in our typology, we would end up with eight broad categories of research design. We don’t see the qualitative-quantitative difference in answer strategy to be as fundamental as the differences in inquiry and data strategy. Each of our four categories can include both qualitative and quantitative designs.
